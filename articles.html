CLIMATE ACTION

Human activities are estimated to have caused approximately 1.0°C of global warming above pre-industrial levels, with a likely range of 0.8°C to 1.2°C. Global warming is likely to reach 1.5°C between 2030 and 2052 if it continues to increase at the current rate.
On model trajectories with no or limited 1.5°C overshoot, global net anthropogenic CO 2 emissions will decline by approximately 45% from 2010 levels by 2030, reaching net zero around 2050. . By limiting global warming to less than 2°C, CO 2 emissions are projected to decline by approximately 25% by 2030 on most trajectories and reach net zero emissions around 2070.

Warming due to anthropogenic emissions from the pre-industrial period to the present will persist for centuries or millennia and will continue to cause new long-term changes to the climate system, such as sea level rise, with associated impacts, but these are unlikely. emissions alone cause global warming of 1.5°C
Robust climate modeling project differences in regional climate characteristics between current and global warming of 1.5°C and between 1.5°C and 2°C. These differences include increases in: mean temperature in most land and ocean regions, warm extremes in most inhabited regions, heavy precipitation in several regions, and the probability of droughts and precipitation deficits in some regions.
